The Model

The model begins with an assumed wolf density in the final modeled ledger year and works backward one year at a time. For each year, it adds back that year’s effective bounty payments, divides them by At (county area in year t, measured in 1,000 km² units), and then divides by the year’s net demographic multiplier, λ(1−m). The result is a reconstructed previous-year density. County area affects bounty-payment density and raw-count display, but the density curve itself is not forced to jump simply because a county boundary changed.

Dt−1 = Dt + (Heff,t / At) (1−mt)λt
Dt = wolf density in year t, reported as wolves per 1,000 km².
Dt−1 = back-calculated density for the previous year.
Heff,t = effective bounty payments in year t: the recorded or imputed bounty count after applying any capped compensation. If c = 0, this equals the bounty count. If c > 0, the model lets some bounty payments replace deaths that would otherwise have occurred, but only up to the estimated non-bounty mortality available in that year.
At = county area in year t, in 1,000 km² units.
λt = annual population growth multiplier before additional non-bounty mortality.
mt = additional mortality rate from non-bounty causes such as disease, strife, starvation, or unrecorded mortality.
c (compensation coefficient): controls how much bounty mortality may displace non-bounty mortality, with a cap so compensation cannot exceed the estimated non-bounty deaths available.
R = human-caused mortality needed to reduce population. This is not an independent model input. The baseline threshold is R = λ(1−m)−1; when compensation is allowed, the diagnostic comparison uses the compensation-adjusted threshold shown in the summary panel.
Data collected from historical Virginia county court records at the Library of Virginia and digitized on FamilySearch.org. Model adjusted from Justin A. Dellinger and Steven Torres, “A retrospective look at mountain lion populations in California (1906–2018),” California Fish and Wildlife 106(1):66–85 (2020).
